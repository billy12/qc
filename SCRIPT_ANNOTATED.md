# Spring Into QC — Presentation Script (Annotated / Study Version)

Not a script to read — a study tool. For each slide: what to hit (in your own words),
and *why that's the choice*, so you can explain the reasoning behind any piece of this
if you're asked, adapt on the fly if you lose your place, or field a curveball
question without it derailing you. Fluency means you could rebuild this pitch from
memory even if every slide vanished.

Cross-references: `HANDOVER.md` (Q&A Challenges 01–07), `PROPOSAL_PLANNING.md`
(homework + open judgment calls), `SOURCES.md` (where every number comes from).

---

## Slide 1 — Title

**Say:** Introduce yourself, name the event, state the one-line frame — "enhancing
engagement beyond the egg hunt."

**Why this framing, specifically:** The Town's own problem statement is about people
leaving *after* the free egg hunts. Naming that gap in your very first sentence tells
the room you understood the assignment before you've shown a single slide of content.
It also previews your structure — everything after this is in service of that one
sentence.

---

## Slide 2 — Who We're Serving (Community Profile)

**Say:** Population, growth rate, income, household size, homeownership, then the
Town's own vision statement and FY26 priority.

**Why lead with demographics before the event itself:** Two reasons. First, it signals
you did homework on the *Town*, not just the event page — a nonprofit-to-municipal
candidate showing up with only event trivia looks thinner than one who understands who
they'd actually be serving. Second, every stat here is chosen to set up later
payoffs, not just to exist:
- Average household size (~3.4) → this is a family event for a family-sized town;
  justifies why retention past the egg hunt matters at all
- 90% homeownership + high growth → residents are invested, not transient; the Town
  has room and reason to keep building signature events
- The vision quote and FY26 priority → this is your anchor for later pushback. If
  anyone questions why this matters, you can point back to the Town's *own* stated
  priority (Quality of Lifestyle) and vision statement — you're not inventing a
  rationale, you're executing theirs.

**Fluency check:** Could you explain *why* you picked these six stats out of all
available Census data, instead of just reciting them? If not, that's the gap to close
before Wednesday.

---

## Slide 3 — Current State & The Gap

**Say:** What the event offers (the full activity list), then the gap (families leave
after the hunt).

**Why list everything the event offers before naming the problem:** Tone matters here.
Town staff built this event — it's currently well-attended (3,500+) and clearly
loved. Leading with "here's what's working" before "here's the gap" reads as
*evaluation*, not *criticism*. That distinction is exactly what interviewers are
listening for when they ask candidates to assess an existing program: can you critique
without being dismissive of the people who built it?

**Why the attendance figure (3,500+) matters:** It converts "some families leave
early" from a vague impression into a concrete stakes statement — you're talking about
thousands of people, which makes the retention problem worth solving in dollar and
attention terms, not just a nice-to-have.

**Q&A tie-in:** Challenge 02, "How do you know families are leaving early? Where's the
data?" — your honest answer lives on Slide 5, not here. Don't over-claim certainty on
this slide; the gap is described qualitatively here and the assumption is flagged
explicitly two slides later. That sequencing is deliberate — state the problem, build
credibility, *then* show your work.

---

## Slide 4 — What the Research Revealed (3 Findings)

**Say:** Raffle loop is broken → activities run in silos → the post-hunt moment is
unclaimed.

**Why three findings, and why this order:** Each finding maps directly to one piece of
your recommendation — this is the connective tissue of the whole presentation, even
though you don't say so explicitly until Slide 6:
- Finding 1 (raffle) → becomes Pillar 2 (Gamify Retention)
- Finding 2 (silos) → becomes Pillar 1 (Redesign the Journey)
- Finding 3 (post-hunt moment) → gets absorbed into Pillar 1's physical redesign
  (the "periphery loop on the return route" design decision *is* the answer to
  Finding 3 — it replaces what was originally a much smaller idea, just stationing a
  staffer at the exit, with a structural fix)

**Why order matters:** Landing on Finding 3 last, with the "30-second window" framing,
is your emotional beat before the recommendation — it's the most specific, most vivid
finding, and it's the one that makes the audience feel the problem right before you
solve it.

**Key phrase for later:** This slide is where you plant "the elements are strong, the
connections are missing" — you'll want this exact framing back in your pocket for
Challenge 06 (the hardest one — "isn't this incremental?"). Your answer there is
essentially: *we're not adding complexity, we're removing friction. That's the job.*

## Slide 5 — Research Foundation (Confirmed vs. Assumed)

**Say:** Confirmed facts (event basics, attendance, vendor fees, Town survey
precedent), then assumed items (exit data, dwell time baseline), then the one-line
data-gap note.

**Why show your assumptions instead of hiding them:** This is the single biggest
credibility move in the deck. Confidently presenting *only* what supports your
recommendation is what a weak candidate does. Explicitly separating confirmed from
assumed — and naming *why* each assumed item is unconfirmed — heads off Challenge 02
before it's even asked, and makes you look more rigorous, not less prepared.

**Why the Town-survey-precedent line matters:** This one fact (the Town already runs
QR-code/online surveys for other things) is what lets you mention a future feedback
mechanism in one sentence, credibly, without it becoming a whole pillar. You're saying
"this isn't a new idea for the Town, it's a known method" — that's what makes it safe
to gesture at briefly instead of building it out.

**Important — scope discipline:** This is intentionally the *only* place the data-gap
idea gets real estate, plus one closing-line callback on Slide 11. It does **not** get
its own pillar slide, its own operational-plan line items, or its own budget line.
That was a deliberate late edit — the assignment is about a *meaningful engagement
enhancement*, and a data-collection mechanism is a supporting idea, not the
recommendation itself. If you feel yourself wanting to talk more about the feedback
survey in the room, that's a sign to redirect back to the two pillars.

## Slide 6 — The Recommendation: Overview

**Say:** State both pillars in one breath, then the one-line "why together" close.

**Why only two pillars, not three or four:** Discipline. You could pitch a dozen
ideas. Two, tightly connected, is what's defensible in a 10-minute room and what's
actually operationally executable at low cost. This slide is also your *roadmap*
statement — telling the audience "here are the two things, in this order" before you
dive into either one, so they're not surprised by the structure.

**Why "Pillar 1 gets them moving, Pillar 2 gives them a reason to keep moving":** This
one sentence is the thesis of the entire recommendation. If you remember nothing else
verbatim, remember this — it's the answer to "why do these two ideas belong together"
before anyone has to ask.

## Slide 7 — Pillar 1: Event Layout & Participant Journey

**Say:** Walk the flow — Entrance A/B → Great Egg Walk → Vendor Row → Central Egg Hunt
→ Animal Races → Periphery Loop. Then design principles, then why it works.

**Why a physical redesign instead of just adding signage:** The original, simpler
version of this idea was just "station a staff member at the hunt exit to point
people toward the next activity." That's a fine idea, but it's a patch, not a fix — it
depends on staff execution every single time, every single wave, all morning. Moving
the *egg hunt itself* to the physical center of the site, with periphery attractions
on the return route by design, means the layout does the work whether or not a staffer
is perfectly on their mark in any given moment. That's the difference between a
process fix and a design fix — and design fixes are more durable.

**Why "Entrance A/B" matters beyond flow control:** It also gives you a natural
distribution point for Pillar 2's punch/bingo cards (mentioned on Slide 8) — the two
pillars share this piece of infrastructure, which is part of why they're presented as
"connected," not just two separate ideas bundled together.

**Honest gap to know before Wednesday:** You don't have a confirmed site map of
Frontier Family Park's exact footprint for the event — you're proposing a *concept* of
circular flow based on the park's known amenities (85 acres, central lake, multiple
zones), not a literal engineered blueprint. If pressed on exact feasibility, the honest
answer is: "the concept is sound given the park's layout; the literal entrance
placement would need a walkthrough with your events team before finalizing."

## Slide 8 — Pillar 2: Gamification & Retention Incentives

**Say:** Grand Prize Drawing (announced early) → Punch/Bingo Card → Golden Eggs → Race
bracket bonus. Close with "four layers, one storyline."

**Why four separate mechanics instead of one bigger prize:** Each one targets a
*different* behavior, deliberately:
- Grand Prize Drawing (announced at open, not sprung at noon) → retention *through
  time* — gives a reason to still be there at 12:15
- Punch/Bingo Card → retention *through space* — gives a reason to visit the
  periphery and the vendors specifically, not just wander
- Golden Eggs → retention *through exploration* — pulls families to corners of the
  site a structured path alone might not reach
- Race bracket bonus → reuses what's *already* running, so it's not new operational
  lift, just better-connected

If you're asked "why not just one bigger raffle prize," that's the answer: one prize
solves for "stay longer," but doesn't solve for "actually see the vendors" or
"actually explore the whole site." You need multiple mechanics because you're solving
multiple sub-problems.

**Honest gap to know before Wednesday:** You don't have a confirmed vendor count to
size the bingo card grid correctly (see `SOURCES.md` — this was flagged as
unresolved). If asked how big the bingo board is, the honest answer is "sized to
however many vendors are actually there — I'd finalize the grid with the vendor
coordinator once headcount is confirmed," not a specific number you'd have to defend.

**Personal experience hook:** This is a natural place to bring in your own background
running signature events serving 500+ people at the Boys & Girls Club — you've built
incentive structures before (even informally) to get kids to engage across multiple
activity stations rather than camping at one. Say so if the moment allows it; it's the
single best "this isn't theoretical for me" beat available in the whole presentation.

## Slide 9 — Operational Plan

**Say:** Walk the five phases — 8 Weeks Out, 5 Weeks Out (promotion), 2 Weeks Out, Day
Of, Post-Event.

**Why a dedicated promotion phase (5 Weeks Out) exists at all:** This was your own
addition, and it's a genuinely important one — everything in Pillars 1 and 2 only
works if people show up expecting it. Teasing the Grand Prize (without revealing it)
and announcing vendors ahead of time doesn't just inform people, it builds
*anticipation before they ever arrive*, which extends the "engagement" idea past the
event day itself into the weeks leading up to it. It's also the phase where you tie
vendor participation back into the Town's own marketing muscle (cross-promotion),
which costs nothing extra.

**Why phase order matters for the story:** 8 weeks out is *decisions* (format, layout,
sponsor). 5 weeks out is *communication* (tell the world). 2 weeks out is *production*
(print, confirm, prep). Day-of is *execution*. Post-event is *proof* (did it work).
That's a clean planning logic you can explain even if asked to justify the exact week
counts — the sequence matters more than the specific numbers.

**Personal experience hook:** This is the slide the handover notes specifically
flagged for weaving in your own background — you already do annual planning and
monthly budget/staffing coordination at the Boys & Girls Club. If there's room, say
you'd bring that same phased planning discipline here.

---

## Slide 10 — Staffing & Budget

**Say:** Redeployment model (no new hires) on staffing; the cost table ($270–$995) and
the sponsor revenue offset on budget.

**Why "redeployment, not new hires" is the load-bearing phrase:** This directly
preempts Challenge 04 ("isn't the handoff staff an added labor cost?") before it's
asked. Say it early and plainly. Every staffing line on this slide should trace back
to an *existing* role doing a *slightly different* task at a *specific, bounded* time
window (15 min post-hunt, 30 min pre-gate) — bounded time windows are what make
"no new hires" credible instead of hand-wavy.

**Why show a cost range instead of one number:** A single number invites "why exactly
that number?" A defensible range signals you understand these are estimates pending
final print-run/vendor quotes — which is honest, and also protects you if asked to
justify a specific dollar figure you can't actually back up yet.

**Why the revenue-offset line matters:** It reframes the entire budget conversation.
"Small ask" is fine; "potentially cost-neutral" is much stronger, because it answers
the unspoken budget-owner question — *does this cost the Town anything, net* — before
anyone has to ask it directly.

---

## Slide 11 — Final Recommendation

**Say:** Recap both pillars in one sentence each, the "looking ahead" line, then the
four success metrics.

**Why the "looking ahead" line survives here, even though it's not a pillar:** It's
one sentence, positioned *after* both real recommendations, clearly labeled as future
scope. This is the right amount of ambition to show without diluting the actual ask —
it demonstrates you're already thinking past this cycle, without making the homework
assignment sound unfinished or hedged.

**Why these four specific metrics:** Each one is measurable with what the Town already
has or what this proposal generates on its own — dwell time (observable), card/raffle
submission (literally counted at the drawing table), vendor satisfaction (existing
survey format), sponsor revenue (a dollar figure, easy to report up). None of them
require new infrastructure to track — that consistency matters if you're asked "how
would you actually measure any of this."

**Closing beat:** This is your last impression. Land the two pillars cleanly, don't
rush the metrics, and stop — let the Q&A open naturally rather than trailing off.
