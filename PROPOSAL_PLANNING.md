## 1. The Three Ideas, Organized

These replace/expand the old "two simple fixes" (raffle + handoff) into a fuller,
three-pillar proposal. The raffle and handoff ideas aren't gone — they're now
components inside Pillar 1 and Pillar 2 below.

### Pillar 1 — Event Layout & Participant Journey
**Core move:** stop treating the site as a set of separate stations; redesign it as one
guided loop.

- Controlled entrances (**Entrance A** and **Entrance B**) — improves child safety /
  headcount accountability, and lets you control the starting point of the journey
- Structured path sequence: **Great Egg Walk → Vendor Row → Central Egg Hunt →
  Animal Races**
- Egg hunt sits at the **physical center** of the site — everything else radiates
  outward from it
- Periphery attractions (bouncy houses, Tot Spot, Color Blast) are positioned along
  the **return route**, not off to the side — families pass them on the way out,
  not only if they choose to detour
- Directly answers Finding 2 from the existing research ("activities run in silos") —
  this is a resequencing of the footprint, not new attractions

### Pillar 2 — Gamification & Retention Incentives
**Core move:** the raffle ticket mechanic already exists (Fix 1 in the old deck) —
expand it into a layered system that rewards families for completing the full loop,
not just picking a race winner.

- **Grand Prize Drawing** — high-value, sponsor-funded, **announced early** (event
  open, not a surprise at noon) so families know upfront it's worth staying for
- **Combined punch card + bingo card** — one card, two mechanics:
  - *Punches* for hitting periphery attractions (bouncy houses, etc.)
  - *Bingo squares* for interacting with specific vendors, staff, and Town partners
  - Completed card = bonus raffle ticket
- **Hidden golden eggs** — separate from the timed age-group hunts, scattered across
  the full venue all morning; each one redeems for bonus raffle tickets
- **Animal race winning mechanic** — the existing race-pick tickets get a
  bracket-champion bonus tied into the same prize pool, so all three ticket sources
  feed one storyline

### Pillar 3 — Addressing the Data Gap
**Core move:** be explicit in the "Gaps" section that the whole premise — *why*
families leave early — is currently an assumption, not a measured fact. Then propose
the fix.

- Name the gap directly: no exit data, no exit interviews, no confirmed reason for
  early departure beyond the Town's own problem statement
- Recommend a **formalized feedback mechanism** — e.g., a QR code / short-link survey
  at the exit — to collect real reasons (logistics, weather, nap schedules, ran out of
  things to do, etc.)
- Frame this as closing the loop for **next year's planning**, not just this
  recommendation — turns "we assumed" into "and here's how we stop assuming"

**Why present it this way:** Pillar 1 gets families moving through the whole site.
Pillar 2 gives them a reason to keep moving. Pillar 3 makes sure next year's version
of this proposal is built on data instead of guesses. That's a clean three-beat
narrative for the "meaningful, not incremental" pushback (Q&A Challenge #06).

## 3. Homework — What to Verify Before You Finalize

Everything below is currently based on inference from public event descriptions, not
confirmed operational reality. The stronger your proposal, the more of this you can
firm up before July 13.

### For Pillar 1 (Layout & Journey)
- [ ] Look for an actual **site map** of Frontier Family Park (park website, past
      event programs, satellite/aerial view) — is a true "circular loop" physically
      possible given the park's shape, or does it need to be a different geometry
      (figure-8, linear-with-return)?
- [ ] Check whether the event **already has one main entrance** vs. multiple — adding
      "Entrance A/B" is a bigger operational change if today there's a single gate
- [ ] Estimate rough **attendance volume** (news photos, past coverage) to sanity
      check whether a single-file "loop" would create bottlenecks with a crowd this
      size
- [ ] Check past-year **photos/video** (Hoodline, Chamber, Queen Creek Independent
      galleries) for where the egg hunt, races, and vendor tents actually sit relative
      to each other today

### For Pillar 2 (Gamification)
- [ ] Find the actual **number of vendors** at the event — sizes your bingo card grid
      realistically (a 5x5 bingo board is a mismatch if there are only 8 vendors)
- [ ] Ask (or find out from McKinna Evans, your vendor contact) whether vendors have
      **bandwidth** to staff a bingo-square interaction on top of their own booth —
      this is a real "added labor" question you'll get in Q&A, similar to Challenge 04
- [ ] Check if the Town has run **raffle/bingo-style programs** at other Queen Creek
      events (Trunk-or-Treat, Fall Festival, etc.) — a precedent strengthens
      feasibility; it also shows you did homework specific to this Town, not a
      generic playbook
- [ ] Consider: for **Tot Spot age (5 and under)**, should the card be parent-carried
      rather than child-managed? Small detail, but shows operational thinking
- [ ] Sanity-check the **golden egg count** — too few and it's a non-event, too many
      and it dilutes the timed hunt's specialness. Look at typical "bonus egg"
      quantities used at comparable community egg hunts for a defensible number

### For Pillar 3 (Data Gap)
- [ ] Check queencreekaz.gov Parks & Rec pages for whether the Town **already runs
      post-event surveys** for other events — if so, name the existing tool (e.g.
      SurveyMonkey/Qualtrics) instead of proposing new infrastructure from scratch
- [ ] Confirm whether Town events already use **QR-code signage** as a norm — if this
      already exists elsewhere in Town programming, frame Pillar 3 as "extend an
      existing practice" rather than "introduce a new one" (stronger answer to
      Challenge 06)

### General
- [ ] Re-read `HANDOVER.md` Q&A Challenge 06 ("isn't this incremental?") — the 3-pillar
      structure is your strongest answer to that now; make sure your verbal framing
      leads with it
- [ ] Once you've done the above, flag which of the "Assumed" items on Slide 5 you
      were able to **move to Confirmed** — every one you convert makes the proposal
      more credible

## 4. Open Judgment Calls

Things I can't resolve for you — worth deciding before the deck is final:

1. **Card complexity vs. 4-hour event window.** A combined punch+bingo card is a
   great retention mechanic, but it's also new logistics for a short event. Decide if
   it launches full-scale in Year 1 or as a **pilot at reduced scale** (e.g., just the
   bingo half first, punch card added Year 2).
2. **Budget ceiling.** Adding signage + cards + golden eggs pushes total estimated
   cost up from the original ~$150–$720 range. Confirm you're comfortable presenting
   a wider range (roughly $270–$1,000 depending on print run) — or scale back scope
   before the deck locks.
3. **How much of Pillar 3 gets a full slide vs. a callout.** The doc above treats it
   as living inside Slide 5 (Data Gap callout) + Slide 9 (Operational Plan), not a
   standalone pillar slide, to protect your 10-minute budget. Confirm you're OK
   presenting it that way rather than as a fourth headline pillar.
